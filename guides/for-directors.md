# VAST for Directors and Mid-Management

## What this means for you

You've seen this: OKRs that feel disconnected from each other. Goals that get rewritten mid-quarter because "the approach changed." Work thrown away because an architectural decision shifted under you. VAST names the layer that causes this — and gives you vocabulary to escalate it.

## The one thing to know

Your OKRs live at three different levels, and VAST names them:

| Level | What it is | Your OKR example |
|---|---|---|
| **Architecture (Outcomes)** | What domains matter | "Customer retention is a priority domain" |
| **Strategy (Use Cases)** | Where to invest | "Focus on onboarding experience this quarter" |
| **Tactics (Outputs)** | What to build | "Ship redesigned onboarding flow by March" |

Most OKR frustration comes from mixing these levels. "Ship onboarding flow" (Output) looks like a goal but it's actually a task. "Improve retention" (Outcome) is the real goal — but without naming the Outcome level, teams default to Output-shaped OKRs.

## What changes

**Your goals get structural context.** Instead of "here are my OKRs" → "here are my OKRs, and here's the Architecture they serve." This makes it easier to push back on goals that don't connect to anything structural.

**Escalation gets a vocabulary.** "This is a Tactics problem" vs "this is an Architecture gap" — naming the level helps your skip-level understand what kind of decision is needed.

**Less surprise rework.** When Architecture is explicit, you're less likely to build something that gets thrown away because an architectural decision changed under you.

## What doesn't change

- How you run your team day-to-day
- Your existing processes (LeSS, Scrum, whatever works)
- Who you report to

## What you'd want to see before buying in

1. **One prevented rewrite** — show me a case where naming the Architecture layer stopped wasted work
2. **A PM who credits this voluntarily** — not because they were told to
3. **No new meetings** — if VAST adds meetings, it's being adopted wrong
4. **Pilot first** — one area, measurable results, then expand

## Questions you should ask your leader

- Who owns Architecture in our area? Is it named?
- Does this change what I'm accountable for, or just how I frame it?
- Can we pilot this in one area before going wide?
- How does this interact with our existing OKR/LeSS process?
- What does "Architecture decision" mean concretely for my team?

## If you want to try it: 2-week pilot

**Week 1:**
- Pick one area where OKRs feel disconnected or rework is frequent
- Write down: what are the Outcomes (Architecture), Use Cases (Strategy), and Outputs (Tactics) in this area?
- Name who owns Architecture decisions here (even informally)

**Week 2:**
- In your next planning or retro: use the vocabulary once ("this is a Tactics fix, but the gap is at Architecture level")
- See if it changes the conversation
- If it does → expand. If it doesn't → drop it. No sunk cost.
