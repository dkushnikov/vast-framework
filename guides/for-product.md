# VAST for Product Leaders

## What this means for product

VAST's original application is product — it was born from a specific problem: product teams making architectural decisions by default because no one else owned them.

## The problem VAST solves for you

You've seen this: a Product Director sequences use cases, makes implicit architectural commitments along the way ("we'll use this API, we'll structure data this way"), and months later the architecture needs rework because those decisions were never made deliberately.

VAST says: **Architecture decisions should be explicit and owned, not made by default through product sequencing.**

## What changes

**Architecture gets a named owner.** Not necessarily CTO — but someone explicitly owns "what is our composition framework" before product strategy commits to use cases.

**Your role sharpens, not shrinks.** Product owns Vision (what experiences we enable, for whom) and Strategy (which use cases to pursue, in what order). Architecture owns the capability framework. These are complementary, not competitive.

**Challenge Flow becomes explicit.** Product challenges Architecture: "this capability framework doesn't serve our Vision." Architecture challenges Product: "this use case assumes capabilities we don't have." Both are legitimate and formalized.

## What doesn't change

- Product still owns Vision and customer understanding
- Product still drives Strategy and use case prioritization
- Product still works with engineering on Tactics

## The AI-first product angle

In AI-first products, Architecture = the composition framework: what AI skills exist, how they compose, what invariants hold. This is the defensible artifact — not the specific model underneath. Product defines what experiences to compose; Architecture defines what's composable.
